Your song is transformative: you're not re-releasing the movie scene, promoting the film, or using the line for its original dramatic/horror purpose. You're turning a random panic scream into part of a surreal, laughter-driven art piece. It's not a straight sample for vibe — it's embedded in your "legendary would you rather HAHAHA" routine. Courts like transformative uses that add new expression, meaning, or absurdity (see cases like the Jersey Boys using a 7-second Ed Sullivan clip as historical reference, or parody cases).
Nature of the original work — The movie is a creative, fictional horror film, so this weighs against fair use (creative works get stronger protection than facts/news).
Amount and substantiality — Strongly in your favor. 5 seconds out of a 90+ minute movie is tiny. It's not the "heart" of the film (no plot spoilers, iconic lines, or memorable music cue). It's a generic exclamation anyone might say in panic. De minimis cases (like short horn hits or tiny audio snippets) often find that such minimal copying isn't even infringement if the average listener wouldn't recognize or care about it as "taking" from the movie.
Effect on the market — Almost certainly none. Your song isn't competing with or substituting for Would You Rather. No one is going to skip watching the movie because of your laughter opus. The rights holder (likely the production company or distributor) isn't losing sales, licensing fees, or value here.

De minimis adds another layer: some courts say truly trivial copying (especially unrecognizable or insignificant in context) isn't infringement at all, even before fair use analysis.Realistic Risk LevelLawsuit probability: Extremely low.
